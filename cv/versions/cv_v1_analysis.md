# CV Audit: What Works, What Hurts, What Is Missing

## 1. What is weak, generic, or reads like AI slop

- The opening label, `Early-Career Full-Stack Developer`, is technically safe but strategically weak. It makes you sound interchangeable before the reader reaches the real signal.
- The summary is a long inventory of tools and intentions. It tells the reader that you want to grow more than it proves why they should interview you now.
- `Core Strengths` repeats the same content already present in the summary and skills list. It adds volume, not clarity.
- The current CV flattens all projects into the same weight class. A local-first RAG platform, insurer API automation, and a static marketing site are all presented with the same visual importance, which hides your strongest work.
- The AI angle is under-positioned. `LLM training and evaluation` is listed, but there is no flagship system showing how that judgment translates into product architecture or engineering constraints.
- The current site copy leans on broad phrases like `real workflows`, `production ownership`, and `not toy projects` without enough technical texture underneath. Senior engineers notice that immediately.
- Training courses occupy more space than they deserve for an engineering CV. Large companies will care far more about shipped systems, architecture choices, and code ownership than short external courses.
- The education section is stale. Saying `In Progress` after graduation is a credibility problem.
- The current public CV is recruiter-friendly in tone, but it is too close to marketing copy. It sounds optimized to reassure non-technical readers rather than convince technical reviewers.

## 2. What is strong and should be preserved

- You have real production experience, not just coursework or tutorial repos.
- The finance, insurance, and accounting work is a strong differentiator because it implies messy inputs, operational risk, and business constraints.
- The document-processing work is credible and rare for a junior profile: PDF extraction, OCR fallback, reconciliation, audit-friendly outputs, and Excel generation are concrete engineering problems.
- You already have evidence of backend ownership: Flask apps, API integrations, SQL-backed systems, Linux deployments, Apache, mod_wsgi, and production support.
- The search / retrieval background is real. Even before the new RAG project, you already had indexing and retrieval concepts in your work.
- Outlier is useful when framed correctly. It should not be treated as your AI credential; it should be treated as the experience that sharpened your judgment around failure modes, evaluation, and structured output quality.
- `various bank statement layouts` is a strong operational detail while staying NDA-safe.

## 3. What is missing for junior full-stack roles

- A sharper full-stack narrative. Right now you look backend-heavy with some web work, not like a junior full-stack developer who can confidently move between UI, API, auth, and deployment.
- Modern web keywords are underrepresented. The RAG platform gives you a legitimate entry point for `Next.js`, `TypeScript`, `React`, and `SSE`.
- Authentication and session handling are not currently surfaced, even though they matter for SaaS-style roles.
- Background jobs, ingestion workflows, and async processing exist in your work, but the current CV does not package them in full-stack product language.

## 4. What is missing for AI / RAG / SaaS roles

- No flagship AI system is described with enough detail to signal serious work.
- Missing terms and concepts: `RAG`, `Retrieval-Augmented Generation`, `embeddings`, `vector database`, `chunking`, `citation validation`, `retrieval scoping`, `local-first`, `structured outputs`, `LLM integration`, `worker process`, `chat streaming`, `hybrid retrieval`.
- The current document only hints at retrieval and LLM work. It does not describe architecture, system boundaries, or safety constraints.
- There is no narrative that connects your document-processing background to AI product work, even though that connection is one of your best differentiators.

## 5. What large tech companies expect that is not present

- A tighter story in the first 10 seconds.
- Clear prioritization of the strongest work rather than a long flat list.
- Evidence of architecture thinking: system boundaries, data flow, failure handling, security constraints, and ownership.
- Stronger signals around quality: testing, structured outputs, validation, auditability, and reliable operational behavior.
- Cleaner discipline in what to remove. Large-company reviewers expect focus. Leaving low-signal training items and duplicated summary material weakens that impression.

## 7. Positioning issue: how you are currently perceived vs how you should be perceived

- Current perception:
  A broad early-career developer with a mixture of backend work, internal tools, websites, and some vague AI exposure.

- Better perception:
  A junior full-stack developer with unusually strong production ownership for his level, focused on local-first AI systems, document-heavy automation, and backend workflows that need to be reliable, auditable, and secure.

- Why that change matters:
  The first framing sounds like a capable generalist who is still searching for a lane. The second framing sounds like a focused builder who already has a technical identity.
